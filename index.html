My honest read on what happened

The price was a step over the market and barely moved+

Based on what the area was supporting for a home this size, the asking price looked like it was sitting just above that range. The harder part is that over a long run on the market, the number barely came down. When the market keeps saying no, a small trim is not really an answer, it just keeps the same result going. The home was not the problem. The number needed a decisive move to where buyers were actually ready to act, and it never quite got there.

A year on the market reads as stale, and that compounds+

The longer a home sits, the more buyers assume something must be wrong with it, even when there is not. After it came off and went back on, that clock did not really reset, so newer buyers saw an old listing and kept scrolling. None of that is a reflection of the home. It is just how time on market shapes what buyers expect. A true relaunch, a new listing number and the right price from day one, clears that and gets fresh eyes on the home.

A newer home competes directly with the builder nearby+

This is the quiet one that catches a lot of newer resales in Cibolo. A buyer shopping this area is also weighing the builder's own homes, and the builder can offer rate buydowns and closing cost help that a private seller usually does not match dollar for dollar. The way to win is not to out-discount them. It is to position your home where it actually beats new construction: a larger floor plan, ready now, upgrades and landscaping already in, and no build wait. That advantage has to be made obvious, not assumed.

Reprice decisively and relaunch clean+

The cleanest reset is a real one: a new listing number and a price moved decisively to where the market is, not another small trim. Pair that with a sharp first photo and marketing aimed at move-up buyers who want more room without the builder wait, and the same home that sat for a year starts competing on a level field with fresh buyers who never saw the older listing.
